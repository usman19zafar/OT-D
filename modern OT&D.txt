\section{Introduction}

Hybrid Human–AI organizations are inherently complex systems, combining human decision‑making, AI autonomy, structural dependencies, and governance requirements. Classical organizational theory provides useful dimensions but lacks a mathematically closed framework capable of capturing AI‑native behavior, uncertainty, and information theoretic structure. This research introduces a new approach: instead of increasing structural complexity, it reduces the organizational system to a simple and unified
probabilistic representation. The development of this framework involved evaluating two distinct normalization strategies. 

The first relied on unit‑interval normalization, where each organizational dimension was scaled to the range $(0,1)$. Although Simple, this approach proved inadequate
for large and heterogeneous organizational setups, as it clipped information, could not represent uncertainty, and was incompatible with information‑geometric tools. The second and final approach introduced a latent probabilistic representation, in which each organizational dimension is generated by a latent variable and mapped to the probability simplex through a softmax transformation. This representation generalizes across diverse organizational contexts, supports mixed data types, and provides a natural foundation for Fisher–KL geometry and stochastic governance dynamics. It also ensures automatic simplex invariance
and enables uncertainty‑aware modeling.

The resulting framework unifies measurement, dynamics, constraints, and
governance into a single information‑geometric system. Measurement is closed
through RKHS‑bounded feature maps, dynamics evolve on latent space via
controlled stochastic differential equations, and governance is encoded through
a Kullback–Leibler potential with strict dissipation. Together, these elements
form a controlled stochastic manifold with explicit Fisher–KL information
geometry and guarantee convergence to a governance‑optimal equilibrium. Despite the depth of the underlying mathematics, the final structure is simple,
interpretable, and empirically testable. It provides the first mathematically
indestructible foundation for AI‑native organizational theory and design.

\subsection{Measurement closure}

Let $\mathcal{U}(t)$ denote the organizational universe at time $t$ (documents, communications, logs, code, metrics, etc.).  
Let $(\Omega,\mathcal{F},\mathbb{P})$ be a probability space and
$\{\mathcal{F}_t\}_{t \ge 0}$ a filtration representing the information available up to time $t$,
generated by $\mathcal{U}(s)$ for $s \le t$.

For each dimension $i = 1,\dots,17$, define a (possibly nonlinear) feature map
\[
\varphi_i : \mathcal{U} \to \mathbb{R},
\]
implemented in practice by LLM-based semantic extraction.

We define the \emph{observable} organizational state as the conditional expectation
\[
x_i(t)
=
\mathbb{E}\big[\varphi_i(\mathcal{U}) \,\big|\, \mathcal{F}_t\big],
\quad i = 1,\dots,17.
\]

Thus, each dimension $x_i(t)$ is a well-defined $\mathcal{F}_t$-adapted process and is, in principle, statistically estimable from data, rather than arbitrarily assigned.  
The LLM layer is operationally embedded in the choice of $\varphi_i$, but the state itself is defined in purely probabilistic terms.

\subsection{State constraint: compositional structure}

To remove scale ambiguity and enforce trade-offs among dimensions, we impose a compositional (simplex) constraint on the state:
\[
\sum_{i=1}^{17} w_i x_i(t) = 1,
\quad
x_i(t) \in [0,1],
\quad
w_i > 0,
\quad
\sum_{i=1}^{17} w_i = 1.
\]

Here $w_i$ are fixed, exogenously chosen importance weights (e.g., reflecting strategic priorities or governance preferences).  
This constraint makes the system identifiable up to a known, explicit weighting scheme and forces any increase in some dimensions to be compensated by decreases (or slower growth) in others.

Define the admissible state space
\[
\mathcal{X}
=
\Big\{
x \in [0,1]^{17}
:
\sum_{i=1}^{17} w_i x_i = 1
\Big\}.
\]

\subsection{Dynamic model and falsifiability}

We model the evolution of the organizational state as a controlled stochastic differential equation (SDE) on $\mathcal{X}$:
\[
dx(t)
=
f\big(x(t), u(t); \theta\big)\,dt
+
\Sigma\big(x(t); \theta\big)\,dW_t,
\]
where
\begin{itemize}
  \item $x(t) \in \mathcal{X}$ is the state,
  \item $u(t)$ is a control/design input (e.g., structural changes, AI deployment, governance actions),
  \item $W_t$ is a $k$-dimensional standard Brownian motion,
  \item $f : \mathcal{X} \times \mathcal{U}_u \to \mathbb{R}^{17}$ is the drift,
  \item $\Sigma : \mathcal{X} \to \mathbb{R}^{17 \times k}$ is the diffusion,
  \item $\theta$ is a finite-dimensional parameter vector.
\end{itemize}

We impose the following conditions:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Lipschitz and growth conditions.}  
  The functions $f(\cdot,\cdot;\theta)$ and $\Sigma(\cdot;\theta)$ are globally Lipschitz in $x$ and satisfy linear growth bounds, ensuring existence and uniqueness of strong solutions for given $(u,\theta)$.

  \item \textbf{State invariance.}  
  The dynamics preserve the simplex constraint:
  \[
  x(0) \in \mathcal{X}
  \quad \Rightarrow \quad
  x(t) \in \mathcal{X}
  \quad \text{for all } t \ge 0,
  \]
  which can be enforced by constructing $f$ and $\Sigma$ tangent to $\mathcal{X}$.

  \item \textbf{Parameter identifiability.}  
  The parameter vector $\theta$ is identifiable from observed trajectories $\{x(t)\}_{t \in [0,T]}$ under suitable excitation of $u(t)$, i.e., distinct parameter values yield distinguishable distributions over paths.

  \item \textbf{Predictive falsifiability.}  
  For a given estimated parameter $\hat{\theta}$ and control $u(t)$, the model yields a predictive distribution for $x(t+\Delta)$:
  \[
  \hat{x}(t+\Delta)
  =
  \mathbb{E}_{\hat{\theta}}\big[x(t+\Delta) \,\big|\, \mathcal{F}_t\big].
  \]
  The model is empirically testable via the requirement
  \[
  \hat{x}(t+\Delta) \approx x(t+\Delta)
  \]
  in an appropriate statistical sense (e.g., mean squared prediction error, likelihood-based tests).  
  Systematic, significant deviations provide grounds to reject or revise the model.
\end{enumerate}

\section*{Appendix A. Classical Organizational Design Dimensions}

This appendix provides mathematically rigorous definitions of the eleven
classical organizational design dimensions commonly used in organizational
theory. Each dimension is expressed as a measurable coordinate of the
organizational state vector


\[
x(t)=\big(F(t),S_p(t),H(t),C(t),P(t),R(t),G_s(t),E(t),Z(t),U(t),T(t)\big)
\in\mathbb{R}^{11},
\]


where each coordinate is defined as a conditional expectation of a
feature map applied to organizational data available at time $t$.
These definitions generalize across organizational types and support
integration into latent probabilistic or information-geometric models.

\subsection*{A.1 Formalization $F(t)$}
Formalization quantifies the extent to which organizational behaviour is
governed by explicit written rules, procedures, and documentation.

\begin{definition}
Let $\mathcal{D}(t)$ denote the corpus of valid internal documents at time $t$.
Let $\varphi_F:\mathcal{D}\to\mathbb{R}$ be a feature map extracting a proxy
for rule density (e.g., coverage, length, or structural complexity). Then


\[
F(t)=\mathbb{E}\!\left[\varphi_F(\mathcal{D}(t))\,\middle|\,\mathcal{F}_t\right].
\]


Higher values of $F(t)$ indicate greater reliance on formal written guidance.
\end{definition}

\subsection*{A.2 Specialization $S_p(t)$}
Specialization measures the degree to which work is subdivided into
distinct roles.

\begin{definition}
Let $\mathcal{J}(t)$ be the set of jobs at time $t$, and let $\tau(j)$ denote the
task breadth or diversity of job $j$. For a decreasing function $g$,


\[
S_p(t)=\mathbb{E}\!\left[g(\tau(j))\,\middle|\,j\in\mathcal{J}(t),\mathcal{F}_t\right].
\]


Higher $S_p(t)$ corresponds to narrower roles and finer division of labour.
\end{definition}

\subsection*{A.3 Hierarchy of Authority $H(t)$}
Hierarchy captures vertical layering and span of control.

\begin{definition}
Let $L(t)$ be the number of reporting levels and $s_k(t)$ the span of control
at level $k$. For an aggregation function $h$,


\[
H(t)=h\!\left(L(t),\{s_k(t)\}_{k=1}^{L(t)}\right),
\]


with $H(t)$ increasing in $L(t)$ and decreasing in average span.
\end{definition}

\subsection*{A.4 Centralization $C(t)$}
Centralization reflects where decision authority is located in the hierarchy.

\begin{definition}
Let $\mathcal{D}_{\mathrm{dec}}$ be a set of decision types. For each decision
$d\in\mathcal{D}_{\mathrm{dec}}$, let $\ell(d,t)$ denote the organizational level
responsible for that decision. Then


\[
C(t)=\mathbb{E}\!\left[\ell(d,t)\,\middle|\,d\in\mathcal{D}_{\mathrm{dec}},\mathcal{F}_t\right],
\]


normalized so that higher $C(t)$ indicates more top‑level concentration.
\end{definition}

\subsection*{A.5 Professionalism $P(t)$}
Professionalism measures the formal education or training required for roles.

\begin{definition}
Let $Y_i(t)$ denote the education or training index of employee $i$ at time $t$.
Then


\[
P(t)=\mathbb{E}\!\left[Y_i(t)\,\middle|\,i\in\mathcal{E}(t),\mathcal{F}_t\right],
\]


where $\mathcal{E}(t)$ is the employee set. Higher $P(t)$ indicates a more
professionally trained workforce.
\end{definition}

\subsection*{A.6 Personnel Ratios $R(t)$}
Personnel ratios describe how employees are distributed across functional
categories.

\begin{definition}
Partition employees into categories $\mathcal{C}$ (e.g., administrative,
professional, direct, indirect). For category $c\in\mathcal{C}$,


\[
r_c(t)=\frac{N_c(t)}{N_{\mathrm{total}}(t)},
\]


and the personnel ratio vector is


\[
R(t)=\big(r_c(t)\big)_{c\in\mathcal{C}}.
\]


\end{definition}

E(t)=\mathbb{E}\!\left[\varphi_E(\mathcal{E}_{\mathrm{ext}}(t))\,\middle|\,\mathcal{F}_t\right].
\]


\end{definition}

\subsection*{B.3 Size $Z(t)$}
Size reflects the magnitude of the organization as a social system.

\begin{definition}
Let $N_{\mathrm{total}}(t)$ be total headcount. Then


\[
Z(t)=\log\!\big(1+N_{\mathrm{total}}(t)\big),
\]


or another monotone transformation.
\end{definition}

\subsection*{B.4 Culture $U(t)$}
Culture represents shared values, norms, and behavioural patterns.

\begin{definition}
Let $\mathcal{C}_t$ denote cultural artifacts (language, symbols, behaviours).
Let $\varphi_U(\mathcal{C}_t)$ be an embedding into a latent representation, and
let $u(t)$ be a scalar index extracted from it. Then


\[
U(t)=\mathbb{E}\!\left[u(t)\,\middle|\,\mathcal{F}_t\right].
\]


\end{definition}

\subsection*{B.5 Technology $T(t)$}
Technology describes the tools and processes used to transform inputs into
outputs.

\begin{definition}
Let $\mathcal{K}(t)$ denote the set of production and information technologies.
Let $\varphi_T(\mathcal{K}(t))$ measure technological sophistication or
integration. Then


\[
T(t)=\mathbb{E}\!\left[\varphi_T(\mathcal{K}(t))\,\middle|\,\mathcal{F}_t\right].
\]

\section*{Appendix C. AI-Native Organizational Dimensions}

This appendix defines six AI-native organizational dimensions that complement
the eleven classical dimensions presented in Appendices A and B. These
dimensions capture the structural, cognitive, and governance-related effects of
AI systems within hybrid Human--AI organizations. Each dimension is expressed
as a measurable coordinate of the AI state vector


\[
x_{\mathrm{AI}}(t)=\big(A_p(t),A_u(t),D_s(t),K_c(t),L_r(t),G_c(t)\big)\in\mathbb{R}^{6},
\]


which integrates with the classical state vector to form the full
17-dimensional hybrid organizational state space.

\subsection*{C.1 AI Penetration $A_p(t)$}
AI penetration measures the extent to which AI systems participate in core
organizational processes.

\begin{definition}
Let $\mathcal{W}(t)$ denote the set of work processes at time $t$, and let
$\alpha(w,t)\in[0,1]$ represent the proportion of each process $w$ executed or
supported by AI systems. Then


\[
A_p(t)=\mathbb{E}\!\left[\alpha(w,t)\,\middle|\,w\in\mathcal{W}(t),\mathcal{F}_t\right].
\]


Higher $A_p(t)$ indicates deeper integration of AI into operational workflows.
\end{definition}

\subsection*{C.2 AI Autonomy $A_u(t)$}
AI autonomy captures the degree to which AI systems act without direct human
intervention.

\begin{definition}
Let $\mathcal{A}(t)$ be the set of AI agents active at time $t$, and let
$\beta(a,t)\in[0,1]$ denote the autonomy index of agent $a$, reflecting its
ability to make decisions, adapt, or self-correct. Then


\[
A_u(t)=\mathbb{E}\!\left[\beta(a,t)\,\middle|\,a\in\mathcal{A}(t),\mathcal{F}_t\right].
\]


Higher $A_u(t)$ corresponds to more independent and self-directed AI behavior.
\end{definition}

\subsection*{C.3 Decision Share $D_s(t)$}
Decision share quantifies the proportion of organizational decisions made or
co-made by AI systems.

\begin{definition}
Let $\mathcal{D}_{\mathrm{org}}(t)$ denote the set of organizational decisions at
time $t$, and let $\delta(d,t)\in[0,1]$ represent the AI contribution to
decision $d$. Then


\[
D_s(t)=\mathbb{E}\!\left[\delta(d,t)\,\middle|\,d\in\mathcal{D}_{\mathrm{org}}(t),\mathcal{F}_t\right].
\]


Higher $D_s(t)$ indicates greater AI involvement in decision-making processes.
\end{definition}

\subsection*{C.4 Coordination Density $K_c(t)$}
Coordination density measures the intensity of interactions between human and
AI agents.

\begin{definition}
Let $\mathcal{I}(t)$ denote the set of Human--AI interaction events at time $t$,
and let $\kappa(i,t)$ quantify the informational or operational load of
interaction $i$. Then


\[
K_c(t)=\mathbb{E}\!\left[\kappa(i,t)\,\middle|\,i\in\mathcal{I}(t),\mathcal{F}_t\right].
\]


Higher $K_c(t)$ reflects denser or more complex Human--AI coordination.
\end{definition}

\subsection*{C.5 Cognitive Load Redistribution $L_r(t)$}
Cognitive load redistribution captures how AI systems shift cognitive effort
between humans and machines.

\begin{definition}
Let $\lambda_h(t)$ and $\lambda_a(t)$ denote the human and AI cognitive load
indices, respectively. Define the redistribution index as


\[
L_r(t)=\lambda_a(t)-\lambda_h(t),
\]


normalized so that positive values indicate net transfer of cognitive burden
from humans to AI systems.
\end{definition}

\subsection*{C.6 Governance Constraint $G_c(t)$}
Governance constraint measures the strength of oversight, alignment, and
compliance mechanisms applied to AI systems.

\begin{definition}
Let $\pi(t)$ denote the AI policy distribution and $\pi^\ast$ the governance
target distribution. Define


\[
G_c(t)=D_{\mathrm{KL}}\!\left(\pi(t)\,\|\,\pi^\ast\right),
\]


where $D_{\mathrm{KL}}$ is the Kullback--Leibler divergence. Lower $G_c(t)$
indicates stronger alignment with governance requirements.
\end{definition}
